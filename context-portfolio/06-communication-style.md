# Communication Style

## How She Communicates
- Straightforward with a little casual and humor mixed in
- Conversational and fast-moving — talks in long, energetic streams
- Self-deprecating about titles and status ("I get paid in words")
- Uses punchy shorthand to describe complex things ("I teach creativity to business people and business to creatives")
- Leads with what she does, not what her title says
- Informal tone; not overly polished
- Narrates work in chronological phases — "we started with... then we... now we..."
- Gets specific about outcomes and numbers naturally ("maybe 10 spec commercials," "an hour or two turnaround")
- Honest and unfiltered about relationships — will say directly when she doesn't respect someone
- Self-aware about her tendencies — names them openly
- Can get frustrated when she doesn't feel heard — will name it directly
- Comfortable naming emotional states without dwelling on them
- Lists tools and tech rapidly with confidence

## Writing Style
- Writes the way she speaks. Casual, direct, not literary.
- Self-identifies as "not a great writer" and is comfortable with that.
- Prioritizes clarity over polish.
- Wants written output to sound like her, not like AI. The worst outcome is something that reads as generated.
- Not trying to sound impressive. Trying to be understood.
- Conversational tone with clear thinking underneath.

## AI Output Preferences
These are hard rules for any AI producing content for or with Elizabeth:

- Direct and plain-spoken. No marketing language, no hype, no filler.
- No emojis. No em dashes. No exclamation points unless she uses them first.
- Don't over-format. Minimal headers, no unnecessary bullet points. Write in sentences when sentences work.
- Lead with the strong claim or the answer, then support it. Don't build up to it.
- When helping her write for an audience, match the register of that audience but keep her voice: clear, confident, slightly irreverent, intellectually serious.
- When helping her think, be a thought partner, not a yes-machine. Push back if something doesn't hold up.
- When building things (code, tools, docs), just build. Don't narrate what you're about to do. Show the thing.
- Default to artifacts or files when the output is something she'll use outside the conversation.
- If she gives a messy brain dump, organize it but don't sand off the edges. Keep the energy.
- She knows her tools. Don't explain what Claude Code is or how Netlify works unless she asks.
- Err on the side of too concise rather than too thorough. She'll ask for more if she needs it.
- **When writing as her or for her:** Keep it casual. Short sentences are fine. Don't elevate the vocabulary. Match how she talks. If it sounds like a press release, start over.

## How She Works With AI on Writing Projects
- Typical workflow: Gathers her own ideas and source material first. Makes a first pass herself. Then brings it to AI (usually ChatGPT) to develop and refine.
- Example: For GSU President's AI Summit presentation, she took her ChatGPT Project on AI and the Creative Industries plus the slide template, worked through ideas with AI, created a draft, then shared it in a meeting with colleagues to edit a Google Doc together. Will take more passes with ChatGPT, then share a closer-to-finished draft with a wider group and get some of them to contribute video.
- Pattern: AI is a collaborator in the middle of the process, not the starting point or the final word. She brings her own thinking first, uses AI to develop it, then pressure-tests with real people.

## How She Works With AI on Coding
- Uses Claude Code and Codex for building (Studio Now work, interactive projects).
- Starts by showing the data she has and describing what she wants.
- Prioritizes getting specs nailed down before coding — because she runs out of Claude credits and needs to use them efficiently.
- Real CS background means she can evaluate, debug, and redirect what the AI produces.
